## Overview

A budget sets a spending limit for a category over a period of time. As you add transactions, Finapp shows how much you have spent, how much is left, and whether you are on track.

Budgets live on their own screen. Open it from the main menu, then use **Add budget** to create your first limit.

## The amount and its rhythm

Each budget has its own rhythm - weekly, monthly or yearly - chosen next to the amount. Enter the amount for one such period (for example 100 a month, or 1200 a year). The page then shows it sliced to whatever timeframe you are currently viewing.

## Week, month or year

The buttons at the top of the screen choose the timeframe everything is shown in. Each budget keeps its own rhythm, so switching the timeframe simply re-slices every budget to it: a 1200 / year budget shows as 100 a month or about 23 a week. The arrows page through time; the middle button jumps back to now.

::note
Re-sliced weekly and monthly figures are approximate - a year is not exactly 52 equal weeks or 12 equal months.
::

## Spent, Assigned, Available

Every budget shows three numbers:

- **Spent** - what you spent in this category and its subcategories during the period.
- **Assigned** - your limit.
- **Available** - what is left: assigned plus any rollover, minus spent. It turns red when you go over.

## Adjusting one period

Tap the **Assigned** number on a budget to set a limit just for the period you are viewing - handy for a one-off month with extra costs. The pencil mark turns highlighted while an override is in place; **Reset** returns the budget to its normal amount. Other periods are untouched.

## The bar and the pace mark

The bar fills toward your limit and turns fully red if you go over. The small vertical mark is your **pace** - where your spending should be by today if you spread the limit evenly across the period. If the fill is past the mark, you are spending faster than planned.

## Parent categories

Budget a parent category and it covers that whole branch - all its subcategories count toward the one limit. If a subcategory has its own budget, it is tracked separately and not counted twice in the parent.

## Group subtotals

Budgets are grouped into expense, income and archived. When a group holds more than one budget, a subtotal line rolls up its spent, assigned and available - a quick read on a whole side of your budget at once.

## Rollover

Choose what happens to a period's leftover:

- **No rollover** - each period starts fresh.
- **Roll over surplus** - money you did not spend is added to the next period.
- **Roll over surplus & deficit** - both unspent money and overspending carry over.

The ↺ line on a budget shows what was carried in from earlier periods.

## Projected

An estimate of how much you will have spent by the end of the period if you keep going at the current rate.

## Committed to recurring

Planned [recurring payments](/en/guide/recurrences) due in this period that have not happened yet. They are set aside so you do not spend money already promised to a bill.

## Safe to spend

The number at the top is your free-to-spend money: what is left across all expense budgets after setting aside the recurring payments still due this period.

## To assign

When you have received income in the period, a second figure appears next to Safe to spend: your income minus everything assigned to expense budgets. It shows how much of this period's income you have not given a job yet. With no income in the period it stays hidden.

## Copy last period

**Copy last period** fills this period's assigned amounts from the previous one in a single tap - a fast start when your budget is much the same from one period to the next. Adjust individual budgets afterwards.

## Move money

Short on one budget? Open its menu and choose **Move money**. Pick another expense budget to pull from - ones with money to spare are listed first - and an amount; the sheet starts with exactly what it takes to bring the short budget back to zero. The amount leaves the source and lands on the destination for this period only.

## Saving toward a target

An expense budget can instead be a **target**: save a set amount by a date. Turn on **Target by date** when adding the budget, then enter the goal and the date. Finapp works out how much to set aside each period - the goal split across the periods left - and shows it as a hint. A target always keeps its surplus, so what you set aside builds up period after period. The budget shows how much you have saved, the share of the goal reached, and the date; once you get there it reads **Funded**.

::note
A target is different from an **income** budget's goal. Income budgets count money coming in; a target sets money aside out of your spending.
::

## Income budgets

Set a budget's type to **income** to track money you expect to receive, such as a salary or a refund. It reads as Received / Expected / Left to receive, and it is a goal rather than a limit: passing it is good - shown in green as **Goal reached** - never counted as "over".

## Currencies

You pick the currency a budget is set in (your main currency by default). It is converted to your current main currency for the bar and totals, and spending in other currencies is converted too - so one budget covers everything, and changing your main currency later keeps budgets correct.

## Archiving a budget

Done with a budget but want to keep its history? Open its menu and choose **Archive**. Archived budgets drop out of your totals and Safe to spend and move to their own section, dimmed. **Unarchive** brings one back - unless an active budget already covers the same category, in which case Finapp asks first and offers to archive the other, so a category is never budgeted twice.

## Adding and editing

Use **Add budget** to create one: pick a category and type, then either an amount with its rhythm and a rollover rule, or turn on **Target by date** for a savings goal. Tap a budget to edit it, or delete it from its menu - your transactions are kept. Editing an archived budget leaves it archived.
